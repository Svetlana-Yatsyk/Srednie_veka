DOCUMENTS AND ASSEMBLIES IN THE KINGDOM OF LEON

IN THE 10-11" CENTURIES

Early medieval assemblies were accompanied by a variety of oral acts and non-verbal
gestures, while charters remained an alien element to them. In the Leonese kingdom of
the period documents were hardly seen as legal proof in dispute settlement since the in-
terpretation and validation of written evidence generally required oral testimony and oath.

witnesses’ signatures. However, it is by no means certain whether the very Visigothic

ancient formulae after the Moorish Conquest. The article aims to consider functions and
status of charters by exploring their performance at the Leonese assemblies of the 10" and
11 centuries. The analysis reveals that documents as material objects could acquire sym-
bolic meaning at the time of proceedings, but generally they were treated as texts. More-
over, authenticity, validity and legality of documents could have been questioned at the
assemblies so those gathered could have had certain ideas of the charters’ legal nature.
However, in the most cases, participants of the assemblies hardly relied on diplomatic
clauses in their examination of written evidence, and such public procedures involving
charters seem to have been moments of celebration, rather than deliberation. It was still
oral and performative context that mostly determined the treatment and the significance
of documents in the 10 and 11" centuries.

Keywords: Kingdom of Leon, early medieval assemblies, history of literacy, dispute
