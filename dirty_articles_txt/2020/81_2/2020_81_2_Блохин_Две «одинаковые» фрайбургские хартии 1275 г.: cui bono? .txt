TWO «IDENTICAL» FREIBURG CHARTERS
OF 1275. CUI BONO?
Two town charters were written in Freiburg in July 1275. Both charters indicate that they
were written by Count Egino II von Freiburg. In 1828, one of the texts was published
(hereinafter referred to as the long draft), the second one (hereinafter referred to as the
short draft) was considered identical and was not published.

M. Blattmann suggested that the short draft appeared earlier. The text was submitted

It is impossible to prove this statement. The ancient legal principle of qui bono —
who will benefit from the new charter — might help to find out who was the author.

The Charters of 1275 continue the traditions of writing charters established during
the Zahringer era. In creating charters, the dukes affirmed their legitimate rule over the
town.

The counts of Freiburg acted differently. The citizens of Freiburg imposed a town
charter to the first Count Egino I (1218-1237), which was called the Stadtrodel. The
Stadtrodel completely limited the legislative initiative of the counts. At the beginning of
his reign, Count Egino II decided to create a new charter in order to consolidate his power.

During the uprising 1248, a «Junior Council of 24 emerged. It controlled the main,
«Senior» Council. Both drafts ignored the new authority body. The fact that both drafts
of 1275 did not mention the new Council shows the attempt of the «Senior» Council to
take revenge for the defeat of 1248. Having attempted to create a new charter, the Count
looked like an ally of the «Senior» Council of 24. Thus, the «Junior» Council of 24 did
not benefit from the new Charter.

In 1275, three political groups fought for dominance in the political arena of
Freiburg. They were the «Junior «Council of 24, «Senior» Council of 24 and the Town
Lord. At that time, none of these forces had superiority over the others.

Keywords: medieval town law, charters, Freiburg im Breisgau, struggle for power among
