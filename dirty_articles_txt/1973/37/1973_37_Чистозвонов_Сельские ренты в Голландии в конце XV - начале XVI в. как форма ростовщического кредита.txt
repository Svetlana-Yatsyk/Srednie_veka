Summary of А. N. Chistozvonov’s Article «Village Rents
in Holland in the Late 15th to Early 16th
Century as Form of Usurious Credit»

The study is based upon tax inventories of the Land of Holland and
West Friesland of 1494 and 1514. The both inventories present informa-
tion of the migrations of the population, its property status, categories
of landownership, village, rent arrears, and forms of rental and usurious
credit.

The practice of selling rents in Holland may be traced back to the
13th century. It was connected with the development of commodity-mo-
ney economy and forms of credit characteristic of it. It became widely

‚ spread in the 14th and 15th centuries in the shape of rent-selling per-
formed by separate peasants. But towns only could sell communal rents
to cover their budget deficite.

The period of the battle of Nancy, 1477, to the early 16th century
was for Holland’s village the time of war devastations. It was the time
of indemnities, bad harvest, floods, murrains. The social and economic
scene like that could not but increase peasant arrears and ruin peasant
households; it stimulated selling communal and private peasant plots.
Under these conditions there appeared and spread a new for Holland’s
village form of usurious credit: selling of rents not only by separate
peasants, but by villages as a whole. In 1494, forty two of the seventy

Whereas the inventory of 1494 explained the practice by wars, deva- ,
station and indemnities, the inventory of 1514 tends to ascribing to the
rents the functions of credit as such.

The late 15th and the early 16th centuries were the period of primi-
tive accumulation and genesis of capitalism inevitably characterized by
expropriation of huge masses of peasants wrought by various means,
including increasing usurious bondage. The selling of rent by villages
in Holland showed on a microscale that new conditions gave birth to
new forms of credit.
