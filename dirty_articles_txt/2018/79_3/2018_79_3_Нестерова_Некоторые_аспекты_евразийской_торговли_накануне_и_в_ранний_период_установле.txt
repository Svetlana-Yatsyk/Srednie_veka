The comparative analysis of these two texts gives many interesting facts. We learn, that
the international trade in the region became more active after the rise of the Yuan Em-
pire, that the Mongols were interested in the intensification of trade relations with other
countries than the Sung dynasty. The comparative analysis of the sources reveals which
goods became more popular under the Mongolian rule and what was happening with
prices and tolls.

On the basis of the sources in question the author investigates the evolution of the com-
modity-money relations in several countries during the 13" century. For instance in Coro-
mandel the coins became the payment means by the end of the century, although Chau
Ju-Kua wrote previously about white cowries being used as the means of payment. Other
interesting theme is the exchange rate of gold to silver during the Sung and Yuan peri-
ods in the East and in the West. Though generally there is not much available informa-
tion about it, the sources inform us, that in the East the gold was cheaper, than in Europe.
Other problem closely connected with the previous one, is the problem of prices. Both
texts include some reports about it. In order to compare prices, the author gives their value
in gold and silver.

Further question concerns state tolls. We find out, that during Chau Ju-Kua’s time Chi-
nese tolls were high and hard to be paid. In later times the Mongols continued to use the
existing system, but they were much more interested in the development of private trade.
The last problem touched upon in the article is the Chinese domestic and foreign trade —
its goods and its counteragents. There is not much material about it in our sources, but it
is clear, that the most important Chinese export during the whole 13th century was silk,
porcelain, rhubarb, while precious stones, camphor, ivory, turtle shells, glass and cotton
were imported.

Keywords: the Mongols, China, international trade, the coins, state tolls, Marco Polo,
