The genesis and structural evolution of the Stonor Manor
in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries
(a study of theemergence of new large estates
in medieval England)
by Yu. R. Ulyanov

The emergence of new estates in England of the fourteenth and fifteenth
centuries has not hitherto been subjected to special investigation. Research
has concentrated on estates formed in the Anglo-Saxon or early Norman periods
and belonging to representatives of the lay and ecclesiastical nobility. This
limited focus of attention has given rise to a distorted picture of agrarian de-
velopment in England in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. In fact, this
period was marked by extensive transfers of land to new owners leading to the
formation of many new estates. About the methods by which these new estates
were formed, their structure and economic organization, and how they differed
from the older estates, practically nothing is known, although this type of
estate undoubtedly exercised a strong influence upon the whole further course
of agrarian evolution.

The Stonor Family rose from small landowners to become great landlords
at the beginning of the fourteenth century, and the history of their landowner-
ship provides rich material for the study of new estates. Mainly from unpublish-
ed documents (Inquisitiones Post Mortem, Court Rolls, Ministers’ Accounts,
etc.) and from published records relating to the Stonor possessions in the Pis-
hill and Bix parishes in South Oxfordshire the author has been able to trace
the history of the Stonor estate. Through the purchase and exchange of lands
attached to a small «patchwork» estate which, though composed of individual
freeholds in other lords’ manors, came to the Stonors by inheritance a small
Stonor Manor was formed at the beginning of the fourteenth century. In the
course of that century the manor grew, till by the early fifteenth century it
had swallowed up a number of neighbouring manors acquired by Sir John
de Stonore, the judge (the history of these manors goes back to the time of the 5
Domesday Book or to the twelfth century): Pishill Venables with the submanor
of the Abbot of Dorchester, Warmscombe, Bix Gibbon, Bromsden, and Bix
Brand; at the end of the fifteenth century Pishill Napper was added.

By the beginning of the fifteenth century the Stonor Manor had become a
large estate, embracing most of the land in two parishes. The formerly separate
manors included in it, which were held from different lords and for different

such «lowly» services as tallage, the entry fine and the heriot — now
became parts of the new manor. The process was reflected (1) in the Inquisitio-
nes Post Mortem, which show that the Crown had to sanction the formation
of the new manor and the absorption in it of several old estates, (2) in the Court
Rolls, recording the take-over by the Stonor Manor of the courts of the

and the extention of its jurisdiction to cover most of the tenants in the large

the amalgamation of the Stonor possessions in the parishes of Pishill and Bix
into one large economic unit, the Stonor Manor, in which the manors and lands
absorbed by it may be detected only in the accounts of the rents paid to the
overlords, and (4) in the rental of 1476, where the final result of the whole process
is easily read.

On the one hand the new Stonor Manor retained characteristic features of the
old feudal estate: it comprised the demesne, the customary and free tenements;
the lord of the manor had a manorial court and enjoyed other feudal rights
and privileges and was himself in turn a tenant of the overlords. On the other
hand the new estate was distinguished not only by its origins (land and manor
purchases), traces of which remained in the succeeding period, and by the com-
plicated system of interconnected tenements and feudal services due for its
several parts, which to some extent hampered the process of unification and
coalescence, but also by certain structural features. Among these were (1) the
character of the demesne: though large, absolutely and relatively (its total
area somewhat exceeded the combined areas of the tenements and constantly
grew as more tenements were absorbed in it), the demesne was a widely scat-
tered aggregate of distinct parts; (2) the small size of the peasant landhold-
ings; (3) the insecurity of the customary tenants’ rights: very few tenants had
hereditary possession of land, the duration of tenure never exceeding three
lives and usually limited to one. These features are explained first of all by
the small-estate structure of the several parts of the new estate — formerly
separate manors having the same characteristics — and also by structural
changes within the manors acquired by the Stonors where the free tenements
or submanors also belonged to them. As a result, either the intermediate tenant
(freeholder, lord of submanor) standing between the lord of the manor and the
peasant, the immediate holder of the land, disappeared, or former freeholds
were joined to the demesne and were now not granted as tenements but let
out as leaseholds.

The author believes that the emergence and consolidation of new large
estates of the Stonor Manor type, which arose as aggregates formed from frag-
ments of old large-estate complexes (large manors, knight’s fees, baronies),
on the one hand promoted the decline and disintegration of the old feudal land-
owning system and, on the other hand, brought about such structural changes
in the old manors included in the new large estate that the older estates
fell apart as units and completely merged with the new manor. This facilitated
the emergence in the latter of pre-capitalist and ultimately capitalist forms
of economic exploitation. Thus, the conditions for the future creation of a
landowning structure adopted to capitalist farming appeared on the territory
of the Stonor Manor as early as the fifteenth century.
