Summary of Yu. R. Ulyanov’s Article
«The Stonor Family Estate in the 14th—13th Centuries:

on the Problem of Genesis of Small Estates in Medieval England»

The author traces the early history of the Stonor family of Oxfordshire,
which joined the group of great landlords of the country in the early 14th
century and produced a number of outstanding figures, such as Sir John de
Stonor, c. 1285—1354, Chief Justice of the Common Pleas, and Sir William
Stonor, 1449—1494.

The study of both published and unpublished sources concerning the
Stonor estate of the period from the late 11th up to the late 13th century has
brought the author to the conclusion that during these two centuries the
Stonors gradually rose from petty freeholders — virgaters of the Domesday
Book times to small landowners possessing at least 91/2 virgates. Unlike the
small estates which sprang up as a result of subinfeudation and the creat-
ion of submanors, the Stonor estate was formed by means of buying up small
free tenements in different manors. The process of extending the estate was
especially intensive in the 13th century, the late 13th in particular.

The characteristic feature of the Stonor estate was its complex  struc-
ture andithe dispersion ofitslands; by the end of the 13th century it consis-
ted of at least 12 separate tenements, varying in size from 4/, to 1 virgate,
situated in the four neighbouring parishes of Pishill, Bix, Watlington and
Pyrton of two Chiltern Hundreds of Oxfordshire: Pyrton and Binfield.
These tenements were held from different lords: from the manor owners and
from their free tenants as well. There were almost no subtenants: the sour-
ces record only one cotter, holding about 2 acres of land.

There are but few direct data concerning the Stonors’ methods of ex-
ploiting their lands, yet the author supposes that the lands were either given
as holdings or for short-term leasehold. It is also quite possible that part
of the lands were directly exploited by the Stonor family by means of hi-
red labour.

As the land property and income increased, the social position of the Sto-
nor family changed. By the end of the 13th century they had so far out-
stripped other well-to-do freeholders, that they reached the level of small land-
owners. This is shown by such facts as that Richard de Stonor was one
of the jurors sworn for Pyrton hundred during the inquisition of 1279; that
for not attending the hundred court he paid a fine as big as that paid by
small landowners; that he married a knight’s daughter and, last but not
least, that his son John de Stonor, a future judge, got the opportunity of
studying law.

In the author’s opinion, the growth of small estates through the buying up of
land and the formation of new «patchwork» manors, as well as the transforma-
tion of well-to-do freeholders like the Stonors into small landowners—a pro-
cess that became more intensive in the 13thcentury with the development
of commodity-money relations — were bound to hasten the commutation
of labour services and lead to the disappearance of the labour-service sys-
tem, and also to the weakening, and, later on, in some cases to the liquida-
tion of old feudal ties and the break-up of the hierarchical structure of feu-
dal landholdings.
