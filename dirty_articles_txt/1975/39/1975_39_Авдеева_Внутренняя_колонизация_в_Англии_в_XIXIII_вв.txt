Summary of К. D. Avdeyeva’s Article «Colonization in England
in the 11th — 13th Centuries»

The study presents an analysis of the cultivation of forests, marshes and
waste lands in England in the 11th to 13th centuries. Conclusions concer-
ning the scale and character of the colonization have been made of the
basis of a quantitative analasys of place-names, reffering to 22 shires
of the 34 described in Domesday Book. The former are situated in various
areas of England and thus make it possible to form a judgement concer-
ning the process in the country as a whole.

The colonization of the 11th to 13th centuries was a continuation of
that colonization of the previous period. By the end of the Ilth c.
man had already settled all over the country, but even approximately, over
was very unevenly inhabited, with the most inhabited areas in the East,
South—East and certain central regions.

The scale of colonization in each particular area was in the first place
determined at that time by cultivation reserves. Principal objects of colo-
nization were marshes, forestes, mountain areas, such as the Pennines,
North Yorkshire etc. The process went rather evenly both in the 12th and
13th cc. (see Map 2), yet it did not find equal reflection in the sources: for
the 12th century they are much scantier, than for the 13th. Despite that,
the 12th century sources list a greater number of parishes than those of the
13th century (621 and 569 respectively). This means, that in the 12th cen-
tury, with a certain reserve of lands more or less suitable for cultivation,
large villages appeared more often, than in later periods. But after the 11th
century, there were few lands suitable for large villages, therefore smaller
settlements, such as hamlets and farms, sprang as prevalent forms of sett-
lement in many parts of England. The number of large parishes reduced
from 52. 1% in 1086 to 29.3% in 1300, with even smaller percentage for new
settlements.

On the whole, from the late 11th to the and of the 13th c., the number of
settlements in the 22 shires increased 2.5 times. Among them appeared a
greater percentage of settlements with «forest» and «secondary» elements
in their names. About 2.5 million acres of arable land were cultivated du-
ring that period, that is ‘/,th of the lands cultivated by the end of the 13th c.
The smallest increase of arable areas was characteristic of the regions
intensively colonized in the 11th c., such as eastern and south — east shires:
about 7 to 8% and 19% respectively. Besides, there appeared hundreds of
thousands acres of meadows and pastures. As a result, in many parts of
the country, cultivation reserves had been exhausted by the and of the 13th
century, the remaining waste lands could barely satisfy village practical
needs. Only areas of non-fertile lands, and, on the other hand, marshes,
moors and barren sandy areas, difficult for tilling by medieval tools, re-
mained uncultivated. Even at that time contours of nowaday landscape
stood out in strong relief: almost all English villages and other rural settle-
ments, as well as most of the towns had already appeared.
